Coffee Stories

The Ancient Origins

Legend has it that coffee was discovered in the ancient Ethiopian highlands by a goat herder named Kaldi. Around the 9th century, Kaldi noticed that his goats became unusually energetic after eating the red berries from a certain tree. Curious, Kaldi tried the berries himself and felt an invigorating rush. He brought these berries to a local monastery, where the monks, skeptical at first, brewed them into a drink. The monks found that this beverage helped them stay awake during long hours of prayer. Word of this magical drink spread, and soon, coffee began its journey across the Arabian Peninsula.

The Rise in the Arab World

By the 15th century, coffee was being cultivated in the Yemeni district of Arabia and soon spread to Persia, Egypt, and the Ottoman Empire. Coffee houses, known as "qahveh khaneh," began to appear in cities across the Near East. These places quickly became hubs of social activity and intellectual exchange. People would gather to drink coffee, listen to music, engage in conversation, play chess, and hear the news. Coffee houses were often referred to as "Schools of the Wise" for the intellectual atmosphere they fostered.

The Coffee Revolution

The 20th century saw coffee become a global commodity. Innovations in brewing methods, such as the espresso machine and drip coffee maker, made coffee more accessible and diverse. The post-World War II era introduced instant coffee, providing a quick and convenient way to enjoy the beloved beverage. However, the real revolution came with the rise of specialty coffee in the latter part of the century. This movement emphasized high-quality beans, sustainable farming practices, and the art of brewing, leading to the vibrant coffee culture we know today.
